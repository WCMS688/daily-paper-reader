# From Physics to Surrogate Intelligence: A Unified Electro-Thermo-Optimization Framework for TSV Networks 

Mohamed Gharib ∗ , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Leonid Popryho ∗ , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, and Inna Partin-Vaisband , Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract —High-density through-substrate vias (TSVs) enable 2.5D/3D heterogeneous integration but introduce significant signal-integrity and thermal-reliability challenges due to electri-cal coupling, insertion loss, and self-heating. Conventional full-wave finite-element method (FEM) simulations provide high ac-curacy but become computationally prohibitive for large design-space exploration. This work presents a scalable electro–thermal modeling and optimization framework that combines physics-informed analytical modeling, graph neural network (GNN) surrogates, and full-wave sign-off validation. A multi-conductor analytical model computes broadband S-parameters and effective anisotropic thermal conductivities of TSV arrays, achieving 

5% –10% relative Frobenius error (RFE) across array sizes up to 15 × 15 . A physics-informed GNN surrogate (TSV-PhGNN), trained on analytical data and fine-tuned with HFSS simulations, generalizes to larger arrays with RFE below 2% and nearly constant variance. The surrogate is integrated into a multi-objective Pareto optimization framework targeting reflection coefficient, insertion loss, worst-case crosstalk (NEXT/FEXT), and effective thermal conductivity. Millions of TSV configurations can be explored within minutes, enabling exhaustive layout and geometric optimization that would be infeasible using FEM alone. Final designs are validated with Ansys HFSS and Mechanical, showing strong agreement. The proposed framework enables rapid electro–thermal co-design of TSV arrays while reducing per-design evaluation time by more than six orders of magnitude. 

Index Terms —Through-substrate vias (TSVs), package model-ing, electro–thermal modeling, graph neural networks (GNNs), surrogate modeling, S-parameters, Pareto optimization, hetero-geneous integration, 2.5D/3D IC design automation

In contrast, through-substrate vias (TSVs) enable three-dimensional (3D) heterogeneous integration by providing short vertical interconnect paths and fine pitch, thereby supporting high-bandwidth, low-latency communica-tion [4]–[7]. However, dense TSV arrays introduce significant electro-magnetic challenges, including reflection, insertion loss, and inter-TSV coupling [8]–[10]. Thermal challenges also arise due to the low thermal conductivity of dielectric liners sur-rounding TSVs, which degrade the effective thermal conduc-tivity (ETC) of the substrate and impact signal integrity [11]– [13]. Accurate characterization of these coupled electrothermal effects is therefore essential during early design stages. Full-wave finite-element method (FEM) tools such as Ansys HFSS provide high accuracy but become computationally prohibitive for large TSV arrays, while approximations such as mesh coarsening reduce solution fidelity. Several analytical and semi-analytical models have been proposed to enable faster TSV analysis during design-space exploration. However, most existing approaches are restricted to simplified configurations, such as signal–ground pairs or small symmetric structures, and therefore fail to capture the behavior of dense TSV arrays [14]–[19]. Moreover, some mod-els neglect parasitic inductive effects, limiting their validity to frequencies below approximately 10–20 GHz [19], [20]. In practical systems, TSVs are typically arranged in asymmet-ric signal–ground configurations with multiple ground return paths. While multi-ground TSV structures have been explored in [21], the proposed models remain limited to symmetric layouts and frequencies below 20 GHz, and often neglect inter-TSV coupling by assuming ground rings around signal TSVs—an assumption that does not hold in dense arrays. Machine-learning-assisted FEM frameworks have recently been explored to improve TSV design optimization. In [22], Ansys Q2D simulations are combined with genetic optimiza-tion to determine signal–ground assignments, but the approach is limited to 5×5 arrays due to the high cost of repeated FEM simulations. Larger arrays are approximated using tiled sub-arrays, which neglect inter-sub-array coupling and can produce optimistic performance estimates (e.g., up to 13 × lower cou-pling in a 10 ×10 array). Other works employ multiphysics co-simulation with particle swarm optimization [23] or rein-forcement learning techniques [24], yet these methods remain dependent on repeated FEM simulations and therefore scale

The electrothermal modeling framework for cylindrical ver-tical interconnects in [29] is adopted as the basis of this work and extended to dense, irregular TSV arrays. Electrically, the original metal-insulator-metal (MIM) formulation for a single-reference ground is generalized to multi-ground signal-ground configurations, enabling accurate modeling of practical TSV arrays with arbitrary signal assignments and multiple return paths. Thermally, the steady-state ETC formulation in [29] is ex-tended with sparsity-aware corrections for partially populated and irregular layouts. This anisotropic treatment preserves physical consistency and avoids overestimating lateral heat spreading under uniform homogenization assumptions.

The proposed framework integrates analytical physics mod-eling, GNNs, and transfer learning to create a surrogate model capable of rapid and accurate S-parameter prediction for TSV arrays. 

A. Analytical Dataset Generation 

To mitigate the prohibitive computational cost of three-dimensional FEM simulations (e.g., Ansys HFSS) during the initial model training stage, the physics-based analytical framework described in Section II is used to generate a large-scale pretraining dataset. Using this framework, 100,000 data samples are generated analytically for TSV arrays with dimen-sions ranging from 3 × 3 to 20 × 20 , and the corresponding RLCG-based netlists are simulated using the HSPICE circuit simulator. The resulting dataset is partitioned into 80,000 samples for training and 20,000 samples for validation. 

B. Graph Representation 

The TSV array is modeled as a graph G = ( V, E), en-abling a flexible representation that naturally supports arbitrary TSV arrangement patterns and variable array sizes as shown in Fig. 5. Graph-based modeling is particularly well suited for this problem, as it captures complex spatial interactions while remaining inherently scalable. As a result, the proposed framework can generalize to TSV arrays larger than those encountered during training without requiring architectural modifications.

E. Transfer Learning Strategy 

While the implemented physics-based analytical model en-ables rapid generation of large-scale training data, it relies on simplifying assumptions (e.g., quasi-TEM behavior) that do not fully capture three-dimensional electromagnetic effects. To achieve analytical scalability with full-wave fidelity, a multi-fidelity simulation-to-simulation (Sim-to-Sim) transfer learning framework is adopted [37]. The approach exploits the strong correlation between low-fidelity (analytical) and high-fidelity (full-wave) physical manifolds. Although the analytical model may exhibit magnitude deviations, it preserves the dominant geometric and frequency-dependent trends of the S-parameters. Accordingly, training is performed in two stages. 

1) Pre-training (Low Fidelity): The proposed TSV-PhGNN is first trained on a large analytically generated dataset (N = 80 ,000 ) obtained from RLCG-based HSPICE simula-tions. This stage enables the network to learn topology-aware relationships between TSV geometry, spatial arrangement, 7

IV. E XPERIMENTS AND RESULTS 

This section presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed framework. First, the analytical electrothermal model described in Section II is validated against full-wave elec-tromagnetic simulations in Ansys HFSS and thermal simu-lations in Ansys Mechanical. This comparison establishes the accuracy and reliability of the analytical framework, thereby justifying its use for large-scale dataset generation during the pre-training stage of the ML model prior to fine-tuning with high-fidelity Ansys data. Second, the predictive perfor-mance of the proposed ML model is evaluated by comparing its S-parameter predictions against Ansys simulation results. Finally, optimization case studies are conducted using the trained ML surrogate model, and the resulting designs are benchmarked against SOTA TSV architectures to demonstrate performance improvements.

F. Scalability and Computational Considerations 

The computational scalability of the proposed graph-based ML framework is discussed in this section. For an N × N

TSV grid, the number of TSVs scales as V = N 2. Since the graph representation models coupling interactions between all TSV pairs, it is treated as fully connected, leading to 

E = V (V − 1) = O(N 4) edges. This quadratic growth in vertices and quartic growth in edge interactions increases memory and computational requirements. Because inference is primarily GPU-accelerated, the framework becomes limited by available VRAM for very large arrays. While CPU-based inference can support larger graphs, it incurs higher runtime. Consequently, a tradeoff exists between array scalability, run-time, and hardware memory constraints. Future work will investigate sparse graph representations and locality-aware edge pruning to reduce memory complexity while preserving modeling accuracy. V. C ONCLUSION 

A scalable electro–thermal co-design framework for dense TSV arrays is presented, integrating physics-based analyti-cal modeling, machine-learning acceleration, and FEM sign-off. The analytical solver computes wideband multi-port S-parameters and anisotropic thermal conductivities for arbitrary TSV layouts. Validation against HFSS using complex-domain RFE shows 5% –10 .6% error for moderate array sizes while reducing evaluation time from ∼ 10 3 seconds (FEM) to sub-second levels. A GNN surrogate, fine-tuned with a limited HFSS dataset, further improves accuracy to below ∼2% RFE with millisecond inference, enabling over 10 6× acceleration for medium-size ( 15 × 15 ) arrays. The framework supports large-scale multi-objective Pareto exploration over geometrical and spatial configurations, eval-uating millions of candidates within minutes and revealing tradeoffs among reflection, insertion loss, crosstalk, and ther-mal conductivity. Final designs are validated through au-tomated HFSS and Mechanical simulations, enabling rapid electro–thermal optimization of dense TSV networks with full-wave verification. 13
