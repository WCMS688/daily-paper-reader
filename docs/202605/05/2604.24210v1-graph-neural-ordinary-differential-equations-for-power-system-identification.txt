# Graph Neural Ordinary Differential Equations for Power System Identification 

Hannes M. H. Wolf 1 and Christian A. Hans 1

Abstract — With the shift towards decentralized energy gen-eration, the increasing complexity of power systems renders physics-based modeling challenging. At the same time the growing amount of available measurement data opens the door for obtaining models in a data-driven manner. A modern method to do so are neural ordinary differential equations (NODEs), offering a framework for continuous-time system identification. Recent extensions, so called graph NODEs impose a structural inductive bias that has the potential to improve generalization of the learned representation. In this work, we employ graph NODEs and extend them with novel ideas to develop message-passing graph NODEs (MPG-NODEs) for identification of coupled systems with heterogeneous node dynamics and edge couplings. This encompasses state-of-the-art machine learning architectures to infer latent representations of unmeasured states from past measurements, local node and edge embeddings to account for heterogeneity as well as an autoregressive scheme to allow for piecewise constant control inputs. We apply MPG-NODEs to identify voltage and frequency dynamics of power systems and compare them to a monolith NODE under identical measurement assumptions. Our case study on the IEEE 9-bus system indicates that the proposed MPG-NODE offers a much more flexible framework with transfer learning options that allow to add or remove powerlines and units with little to no retraining. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

In power systems, dynamical models of frequency and voltage transient behaviour are essential for stability analysis, control design and prediction. Traditionally such models are derived from first principles. However, due to increasing number of distributed generation units, systems become more complex, which renders physics-based modeling approaches challenging. At the same time, the growing availability of measurement-data enables the use of data-driven system identification. Graph neural networks (GNNs) and neural ordinary dif-ferential equations (NODEs) are two data-driven approaches that align well with power systems. NODEs provide a natural framework for identifying continuous-time systems. In recent works, e.g., [1]–[3], they have been applied to identify power system dynamics. However, aforementioned studies require synchronized voltage phase angle measurements, which can be hard to obtain in practice. More recent work [4] illustrates how to drop this requirement by inferring latent phase angle representations from a sequence of realistic observables. GNNs can leverage power systems’ inherent network struc-ture. Recent works [5]–[8] have employed spatio-temporal

However, these models are discrete time forcasting architectures that lack control inputs. Furthermore, many GNN approaches again assume availibility of synchronized phasor measurements which can be hard to obtain in practice. In this work we combine the continuous-time formulation of NODEs and the structural inductive bias from GNNs to propose message passing graph NODEs (MPG-NODEs) for power system identification. In general, the idea is to repre-sent node dynamics and edge couplings by neural networks, that are shared across all nodes and edges in a known graph topology. This has the potential to improve generalization and sample efficiency, thereby facilitating training and pro-viding a flexible model for transfer learning under topology changes. While our approach is fundamentally based on [9], it is also related to [10], [11] which explicitly consider heterogeneous node dynamics in neural graph dynamical models. More specifically, Huang et. al [11] propose graph NODEs together with graph encoders to obtain environment embeddings to condition shared node dynamics, as well as fully latent initial states from global measurements. Allier et. al [10] propose per node embeddings, that act as ad-ditional inputs to the neural network (NN) and condition the node dynamics. Note that [10] assumes full state mea-surements. In our approach, we adopt the idea of per node embeddings from [10]. However, we use continuous-time formulations that employ NODEs instead of state derivative predictors. Furthermore, our approach does not require full state measurements and instead infers unmeasured states from past local measurements via temporal convolutional networks (TCNs). As opposed to [11], we use persistent node embeddings instead of sample specific embeddings obtained from past measurements to account for heterogeneity among nodes. Furthermore, our initial state encoder uses only lo-cal observations instead of global ones. Also, we preserve measured states of the original system in the state-space of the NODE, which helps to regularize the model in a physics-inspired manner. Due to their application domain, both approaches [10] and [11] do not consider heterogeneous edge dynamics or control inputs. As opposed to that we equip our model with learnable edge embeddings and control inputs as additional arguments to the message passing function and the node dynamics, respectively. Overall this allows us to move more closely to the general nonlinear state model descriptions that are widely considered in the control community.

Our contributions are as follows: 1) We develop a novel control-oriented message passing graph neural ordinary dif-ferential equation (MPG-NODE) for the identification of coupled systems with local dynamics that allows for heter-geneous node dynamics and edge couplings through learned node and edge embeddings. Our model uses local past observations to infer partially latent initial states from local historic observations. Furthermore, our model accounts for discontinuous control inputs by unrolling the model in an autoregressive fashion. 2) We apply our approach to identify the dynamics of power systems, leveraging their natural graph structure as an inductive bias for the identification. Specifically, we infer latent phase-angle representations from local historic observations, thereby avoiding the need for synchronized phasor measurements. The learned embed-dings allow for hetergeneous generator dynamics and line couplings. 3) We compare the proposed MPG-NODE to a monolith NODE baseline under identical measurement assumptions and analyze the benefits and limitations of both approaches on the IEEE 9-bus system. The paper is structured as follows. Section II introduces basic notation and fundamental neural architectures. We then proceed with the system modeling in Section III and the proposed MPG-NODE in Section IV. Section V presents the case study on the IEEE 9-bus system. Section VI concludes the paper. II. C. Neural Structures 

For the initial state encoder hθh , we use TCNs. For each node i ∈ V, from the historic observations we encode a sequence through multiple residual blocks as described in Section II-B.2. To the last time instance of the output sequence, we apply a projection head, i.e., a linear layer, to obtain the latent initial state. For the node dynamics fθf and the message passing function mθm , we use fully connected MLPs. We assume that all measured outputs are also states of the system. Aligning with this and (17), the output function is assumed to be known and is thus just a selector of the respective states, i.e., y(i)(t) = gθg (x(i)(t)) = πy (x(i)(t)) of each node.

D. Unrolling the model 

In control problems, the control input u(t) usually varies over time. Therefore, the continous time interval [ts, t e) of (13) can contain discontinuous changes in u over the time horizon. We therefore employ an autoregressive prediction scheme. Consider the sample times ts, t s+1 , . . . , t s+K ∈

[ts, t e), where K are the prediction steps. Then, we proceed as follows: At time ts, we encode the initial state from H

past measurements of u and y as described in Section IV-B and solve the IVP to obtain x(i)(ts+1 ). For subsequent time intervals [ts+k, t s+k+1 ), k = 1, . . . , k = K − 1,we use the solution x(i)(ts+k) as the initial state for the IVPs and condition fθf on u(i)(ts+k) to find x(i)(ts+k+1 ).That way, we recursively solve IVPs to obtain predictions 

ˆy(i)(ts+1 ), . . . , ˆy(i)(ts+K ). An illustration of the overall un-rolled prediction model is shown in Figure 1.

F. Changing the topology 

As all functions in the MPG-NODEs are shared, the topology of the model can be easily modified. This allows us to transfer previously learned models to scenarios, where the topology has changed. Nonetheless, as each node and edge have their own embedding, it is necessary to choose the embeddings for a added node or edge. This can be done, e.g., by choosing it equivalent to the embedding of a certain node or edge with known or inferred similarity. Except for that, only the adjacency information in form of each nodes neighbors Ni ∀i ∈ V has to be updated. Removing nodes or edges solely requires updating the adjacency information. Note that in all cases accurate predictions are not guaranteed and retraining on data from the altered system may be nec-essary. However, retraining effort is typically much smaller than training an entirely new model from scratch. V. C ASE STUDY

B. Results 

We first compare the prediction accuracy of the MPG-NODE to a baseline monolith NODE of type (12). A more thorough description of this baseline model can be found in [4]. Following up, we conduct an example transfer learning experiment to analyze the flexibility of the MPG-NODE under topology changes. 

1) Comparison vs. Baseline: In what follows, we compare the MPG-NODE to the monolith NODE. For this, we anaylze the root-mean-square-error (RMSE) of the prediction of the two models on the evaluation dataset D4. With the chosen K,we obtain a prediction of the voltages and frequencies over 

5 s after a step change in the control input. Figure 3 shows the distribution of the RMSE, i.e., the root of (18), separated into voltages and frequencies. For both the frequency, as well as for the voltage, the median RMSE of the MPG-NODE is almost a magnitude higher than that of the monolith NODE. Figure 4 shows an example prediction of the system trajectories of the MPG-NODE and the monolith NODE for a single sample of the evaluation dataset. While the monolith NODE can accurately reproduce the true trajectories of the system, the MPG-NODE shows slight steady state errors. That being said, the MPG-NODE still captures the system dynamics, e.g., modes and gains, reasonably well. Even though leveraging the graph structure as an inductive bias has the potential to improve sample efficiency and training, we could not observe such an effect in our experi-ments. This can have be due to several reasons. Firstly, the encoder of the MPG-NODE only uses local past information while the monolith NODE can utilize global information. If observability conditions are weak, the encoder can fail to find an accurate latent representation of the phase angle leading to inaccurate predictions. It has been analyzed in [4] that an inadequately learned latent representation of the phase angle can significantly deteriorate the prediction accuracy. This could potentially be overcome by incorporating semi-local past features from neighboring nodes into the encoder. Secondly, dense monolith networks can capture global corre-lations of electrical quantities at each node more efficiently. Modes of different generators are likely globally observed and imposed structure may hinder to identify them efficiently. However, the MPG-NODEs is still sufficiently accurate for most power system applications.

Opposed to that, the structure of the monolith is fixed and likely requires rebuilding and retraining an entire model following topology changes. VI. C ONCLUSION 

In this study, we developed a novel graph NODE for het-erogeneous node dynamics and edge couplings to identify the coupled dynamics of power systems. The model was trained on data from the IEEE 9-bus system and showed reasonable prediction accuracy. While the model did not reach the same level of accuracy as a monolith NODE, it was shown to offer a much more flexible structure that allows for easy adaptation to changes in the system topology: the MPG-NODE could be successfully adapted to a system with an added node and edge with only a fraction of the original training data and effort. In future work we want to investigate potential improvements in the prediciton accuracy by employing semi-local encoders, as well as physics-inspired regularization in the edge couplings.
