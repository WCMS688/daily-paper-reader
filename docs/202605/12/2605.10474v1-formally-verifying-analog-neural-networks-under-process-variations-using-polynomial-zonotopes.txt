Abstract —Analog neural networks are gaining attention due to their efficiency in terms of power consumption and processing speed. However, since analog neural networks are implemented as physical circuits, they are highly sensitive to manufacturing process variations, which can cause large deviations from the nominal model. We present a polynomial-based model that resembles the performance of the neuron circuit under process variations. Then, we formally verify the behavior of the circuit-level model using reachability analysis with polynomial zonotopes, thus, avoiding conventional, time-consuming Monte Carlo simulations. We evaluate our proposed verification approach on three different datasets, verifying both fully-connected and convolutional analog neural networks. Our experimental results confirm the effectiveness of our verification approach by reducing the verification time from days to seconds while enclosing 99% of the variation samples. 

Index Terms —Analog neural networks, formal verification, set-based computing, energy-efficient computing, AI hardware. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The growing demand for artificial neural networks has driven the need for low-power hardware solutions, such as FPGA accelerators, in-memory computing, and CMOS-based inference circuits [1, 2]. This facilitates the deployment of neural networks in Internet of Things (IoT) devices, such as sensors, mobile devices, and wearable technology, where power consumption is a critical concern [3, 4, 5]. Analog inference hardware demonstrates efficiency in terms of processing speed, energy, and chip area. However, the design and verification of analog devices is challenging due to their vulnerability to perturbations such as noise, mismatch, and process variations [6, 7, 8]. To verify the functionality of analog neural networks, they must be fully validated under these variations to ensure safety, which is usually performed by running time-consuming Monte Carlo simulations [9]. In this work, we focus on analyzing and verifying the characteristics of the analog neural network influenced by varying process parameters. 

A. Related Work 

Modeling and analysis of the influence of varying process parameters on analog circuits have been investigated extensively [10, 11, 12, 13]. However, recent advances in the design and implementation of analog neural networks have opened up new challenges that need to be further addressed: During semiconductor device fabrication, variations, such as gate width, threshold voltage, channel length, and oxide

It is well known that perturbations can significantly impact the accuracy of conventionally implemented neural networks [15], and similar effects have also been observed for process variations on analog neural networks [7, 8, 16, 17]. Hence, modeling the behavior of analog inference circuits under these variations is crucial to ensure the safety of these circuits and improve their design and robustness [18]. The emerging modeling methodologies either rely on device physics or machine learning engines [19]. Since developing accurate physics-based models is a challenging and time-consuming task, many works adopt an analytical approach to quantify the parameter variations. For example, machine learning has been utilized for modeling analog circuits under process variations [12]. While the proposed model provides the capability of identifying the sources of variability in the circuit, it uses a single-transistor model, which is insufficient to reflect the full system behavior and therefore cannot be extended to a system-level modeling approach. Other available analog neural network implementations employ Monte Carlo simulations to analyze the behavior of the network under process variations [9, 20], while being incapable of providing a system-level verification approach that is adaptive to different network architectures. Recently, it was shown that system-level verification of analog neural networks under device mismatch can be achieved by utilizing formal neural network verification [18]. Neural network verification can provide formal guarantees of the prediction of a neural network under perturbations, where usually conventionally implemented neural networks are considered [21, 22]. These guarantees are obtained by rigorous mathematical proofs that enable reasoning about the safety of a neural network under perturbation [23]. Unfortunately, these proofs are usually difficult to obtain for large neural networks as it has been shown that verifying certain properties is NP-hard [24]. Thus, modern verifiers introduce relaxations to remain computationally feasible, where both optimization-based approaches [25, 26, 27, 28] and approaches based on reachability analysis [29, 30, 31, 32] are used.

B. Sensitivity Modeling 

To better understand the impact of varying process parameters on our analog inference circuit, we run a sensitivity analysis with an underlying real process based on the PSP transistor model to identify the parameters that have the most significant impact on the circuit performance. As shown in Fig. 2, the neuron circuit contains both NMOS and PMOS transistors operating in the subthreshold region, which are subject to more than 50 variable model parameters depending on the used technology. As modeling the circuit behavior using such a large number of parameters is infeasible, we need to limit our modeling approach to reduce the model complexity and make it scalable for larger inference circuits. We run Monte Carlo sampling with process variations on the neuron netlist (Fig. 2), and analyze the output current’s variance contribution from all the process parameters. Afterwards, we choose the parameters with the highest contribution as the dominant parameters. For different neuron topologies, this step is repeated to identify the new dominant parameters. For the current neuron topology, the behavior can be well described using two dominant parameters, 

ϕ1 and ϕ2, which represent two parameters influencing the surface potential of the NMOS transistor, which affects the threshold voltage and the overall transistor behavior. While 

ϕ1, ϕ 2 greatly influence NMOS transistors, they only have a negligible effect on PMOS transistors in the upper current mirror in Fig. 2, since the current mirror topology mitigates the impact of surface potential variations. B. Bounding Process Variations 

Process variations can be bounded by following the general verification procedure described in Sec. II-D. We start the verification by propagating the input set eH0 = X through the first layer according to (6). In contrast to the general procedure, the weights fW1 of linear layers (7) are no longer matrices but sets fW1 due to the uncertainties introduced in (9). This polynomial model (7) enables us to represent fW1 as polynomial zonotopes, where the noise symbols d1, d 2, d 3 become (scaled) dependent factors α1, α 2, α 3, as given in Def. 2. Given eH0 and fW1, their multiplication can be computed using (3). An example enclosure of the first linear layer is visualized in Fig. 5, where the enclosure is shown with respect to the main process parameters ϕ1, ϕ 2. As these two parameters describe the behavior well, i.e., low variance for each ( ϕ1, ϕ 2)-pair, the enclosure barely has to be enlarged in the z-direction to enclose all points. Thus, the remaining process variations barely influence the final outcome. Please note that the enclosure is represented using a single set eH1 and is only split for visualization purposes. As we proceed to propagate the set through the entire network (6), we repeatedly multiply fWk onto the current set 

eHk−1, k ∈ [κ], and enclose the ReLU nonlinearities using standard techniques (Prop. 1), until we obtain an enclosure of the output set eY. We can then use eY to verify given specifications (5). V. E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS 

To evaluate our proposed modeling approach, we use three different classification datasets: Wisconsin Breast Cancer [39], Iris [40], and MNIST [41]. We simulate our analog inference circuit and run Monte Carlo simulations in Cadence Spectre [42] on a workstation with dual Intel Xeon E5-2683 v4 CPUs ( 16 cores each). We run the set-based modeling approach implemented in MATLAB using the CORA toolbox [43] on a home computer with an Intel Core i7-1165G7 CPU. To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed modeling approach for each dataset, we compare it with a Monte Carlo simulations of 1, 000 

samples. If not stated otherwise, all figures and tables are with respect to an enclosure of 3σ (9). 

A. Main Results 

We report the required time to analyze the circuit under process variations in Tab. I. While the Monte Carlo simulations require hours for the Iris and Breast Cancer examples and up to a day for the MNIST CNN example, the proposed approach runs in a few seconds. It is worth mentioning that we employ an additional setup time of 12 minutes to extract the parametric models by running Monte Carlo simulations on a single neuron for 64 different weight values for positive and then negative input individually. However, this is only required once and can be reused for all architectures. As noted from Tab. I, we are able to enclose an average of 99% of the output variation samples, as expected from the enclosed number of Monte Carlo samples in Fig. 3 and Fig. 4. While the considered models are relatively small, we want to stress that this is not a limitation of our proposed verification approach [21], but rather that larger models are not robust enough against process variations and thus difficult to implement as analog circuits. For example, the Breast Cancer neural network consists of three layers, and we have already seen instabilities for such sizes. However, our verification approach can drastically reduce the design cycle of these larger models by avoiding the time-consuming Monte Carlo simulations. Using Tab. II, we can quickly assess the expected performance of the manufactured circuits. For example, circuits deviating up to 2σ retain a verified accuracy competitive with the accuracy of the nominal model, meaning that ≈ 95% of the circuits can be used in production and only ≈ 5% may be faulty. Moreover, the results highlight the need for a more robust circuit design to obtain fewer faults ( 3σ), and any new circuit design can quickly be validated as demonstrated in Tab. In this paper, we present a scalable modeling approach for analog neural networks that incorporates process variations. We have developed a polynomial-based model by analyzing the neuron circuit under varying process parameters on the transistor level. The model is then deployed in a system-level formal verification approach that encloses the variations in the output of each layer using reachability analysis. We evaluated our approach on three classification benchmarks with different network architectures, including convolutional neural networks, encompassing most of the output variations. In contrast to Monte Carlo simulations, which are not feasible for deeper neural networks (already up to a day for MNIST CNN), our verification approach runs in a few seconds on a normal home computer. Thus, it can be utilized to optimize the inference circuit design and robustness towards process variations, significantly speeding up the design cycle. In future work, we plan to integrate other sources of variation, such as device mismatch and noise, and extend our approach to larger inference circuits. ACKNOWLEDGMENT
